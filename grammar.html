Grammar Cheat Sheet

Sentence Structure 🏛️

Unlike English (Subject-Verb-Object), Japanese follows an SOV (Subject-Object-Verb) blueprint. The verb must always sit at the end of the sentence.

Structure Example:

English: I eat an apple.
Japanese Blueprint: Subject は Object を Verb。
In Action: 私 は りんご を 食べます。
(Watashi wa ringo wo tabemasu)

Verb Conjugation (Formal) ⚙️

Group 1: U-Verbs (Drop -u, add -i base)

Drop final dictionary vowel sound and swap to 'i' variant before attaching endings.
• Iku (Go): Ik + masu = Ikimasu

Group 2: Ru-Verbs (Drop -ru)

Simply remove the final ru and add the suffix.
• Taberu (Eat): Tabe + masu = Tabemasu
FormEndingExample (Iku)
Pres. Positive+masuIkimasu (Go)
Pres. Negative+masenIkimasen (Don't Go)
Past Positive+mashitaIkimashita (Went)
Past Negative+masen deshitaIkimasen deshita

Adjective Conjugation 🎨

I-Adjectives (Drop final -i)

Drop final -i character before joining negative and past variations.
• Oishii: Oishi + kunaidesu = Oishikunaidesu

Na-Adjectives (Keep dictionary word)

Treat base dictionary entry as a noun; append variations directly onto it.
• Shizuka (Quiet): Shizuka + deshita = Shizukadeshita (Note: Exceptions like Kirei look like i-adj but conjugate as na-adj!)

Particles Masterlist & Critical Nuances 🗺️

Particle Key Roles & Critical Nuances Contextual Examples
は (wa)
  • Topic Marker: Identifies the main overarching theme of the sentence.
  • Contrast: Stresses a difference or exclusion between two items.
 Kyou wa samui desu.
(As for today, it is cold.)

Ocha wa nomimasu ga, koohii wa nomimasen.
(I drink tea, but as for coffee, I don't drink it.)
が (ga)
  • Subject Marker: Marks the specific grammatical subject acting out the verb.
  • New Info: Reports sudden events or natural phenomena.
  • Internal State: Used with verbs of ability/need (wakarimasu) or adjectives of desire (suki).
  • Conjunction: Connects clauses meaning "but".
 Ame ga furimasu.
(Rain falls. [Natural phenomenon])

Watashi wa ringo ga suki desu.
(I like apples. [Internal desire])

Takai desu ga, kaimasu.
(It's expensive, but I will buy it.)
を (wo)
  • Direct Object: Marks the target item receiving an intentional transitive action.
  • Space of Motion: Marks a path, route, or exit point through which something moves or leaves.
 Hon を yomimasu.
(I read books.)

Kouen を sanpo shimasu.
(I walk through the park.)

Ie を demashita.
(I left the house.)
に (ni)
  • Static Location: Point of existence (used with iru / aru).
  • Specific Time: Pairs strictly with numerical calendar dates or clock hours.
  • Destination: Landing point of a directional action (such as the destination of iku or the recipient target of au).
  • Indirect Object: Marks the recipient receiving an action ("to someone").
 Nihon に imasu.
(I am in Japan.)

Shichiji に okimasu.
(I wake up at 7:00.)

Gakkou に ikimasu.
(I go to school.)

Tomodachi に aimashita.
(I met a friend.)
で (de)
  • Active Location: Location where an explicit action or event takes place.
  • Tool/Means: Resource, physical tool, or vehicle used to accomplish an action.
  • Constraints: Marks total limits of cost or time (e.g., in total).
 Uchi で tabemasu.
(I eat at home.)

Basu で ikimasu.
(I go by bus.)

Zenbu で sen-en desu.
(In total, it is 1,000 yen.)
へ (he)
  • Directional Path: Focuses on the trajectory heading toward an area (pronounced 'e').
 Kyoto へ mukaimasu.
(I am heading toward Kyoto.)

Nihon へ no tabi.
(The journey toward Japan.)
と (to)
  • Exhaustive Linker: Connects nouns in a strict, closed list ("and").
  • Companion: Specifies who you do an action with ("with").
  • Quotation Marker: Encloses spoken thoughts or quotes before verbs like omou.
 Pen と kami.
(A pen and paper.)

Tomodachi と hanashimashita.
(I talked with a friend.)

Ashita wa ame da と omoimasu.
(I think that it will rain tomorrow.)
の (no)
  • Possession: Shows ownership, placement, or noun modifications.
 Anata の kuruma.
(Your car.)

Nihongo の sensei.
(A teacher of Japanese.)
