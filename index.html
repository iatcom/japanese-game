Sentence Structure 🏛️

Unlike English (Subject-Verb-Object), Japanese follows an SOV (Subject-Object-Verb) blueprint. The verb must always sit at the end of the sentence.

Structure Example:

English: I eat an apple.
Japanese Blueprint: Subject は Object を Verb。
In Action: 私 は りんご を 食べます。
(Watashi wa ringo wo tabemasu)

Verb Conjugation (Formal) ⚙️

Group 1: U-Verbs (Drop -u, add -i base)

Drop final dictionary vowel sound and swap to 'i' variant before attaching endings.
• Iku (Go): Ik + masu = Ikimasu

Group 2: Ru-Verbs (Drop -ru)

Simply remove the final ru and add the suffix.
• Taberu (Eat): Tabe + masu = Tabemasu
FormEndingExample (Iku)
Pres. Positive+masuIkimasu (Go)
Pres. Negative+masenIkimasen (Don't Go)
Past Positive+mashitaIkimashita (Went)
Past Negative+masen deshitaIkimasen deshita

Adjective Conjugation 🎨

I-Adjectives (Drop final -i)

Drop final -i character before joining negative and past variations.
• Oishii: Oishi + kunaidesu = Oishikunaidesu

Na-Adjectives (Keep dictionary word)

Treat base dictionary entry as a noun; append variations directly onto it.
• Kirei: Kirei + deshita = Kireideshita

Particles Masterlist & Critical Nuances 🗺️

Particle Key Roles & Critical Nuances Contextual Examples
は (wa)
  • Topic Marker: Identifies the main overarching theme or setting of the sentence.
  • Contrast: Stresses a strong difference or exclusion between two items.
 Kyou wa samui desu.
(As for today, it is cold.)

Ocha wa nomimasu ga, koohii wa nomimasen.
(I drink tea, but as for coffee, I don't drink it.)
が (ga)
  • Subject Marker: Marks the specific grammatical subject acting out the verb.
  • New Info / Raw Facts: Reports spontaneous observations, sudden events, or natural phenomena.
  • Internal State / Ability: Automatically used with verbs of ability/need (wakarimasu, irimasu) or adjectives of desire/liking (suki, hoshii).
  • Conjunction: Connects clauses with the meaning of "but" or "however".
 Ame ga furimasu.
(Rain falls. [Natural phenomenon])

Watashi wa ringo ga suki desu.
(I like apples. [Internal desire])

Takai desu ga, kaimasu.
(It's expensive, but I will buy it.)
を (wo)
  • Direct Object: Marks the target item directly receiving an intentional transitive action.
  • Space of Motion: Marks a path, route, or exit point through which something moves, transits, or leaves (verbs like walk, turn, cross, leave).
 Hon を yomimasu.
(I read books.)

Kouen を sanpo shimasu.
(I walk through the park.)

Ie を demashita.
(I left the house.)
に (ni)
  • Static Location: Point of existence for items or people (used exclusively with iru / aru).
  • Specific Time: Pairs strictly with specific numerical calendar dates or clock hours.
  • Destination Target: Ultimate destination or final landing point of a directional action.
  • Indirect Object / Agent: Marks the person receiving an item ("to someone") or the active agent doing the deed in passive/causative phrases.
 Nihon に imasu.
(I am in Japan.)

Shichiji に okimasu.
(I wake up at 7:00.)

Gakkou に ikimasu.
(I go to school.)

Tomodachi に hon wo agemashita.
(I gave a book to my friend.)
で (de)
  • Active Location: Marks the physical location where an explicit action or event takes place.
  • Tool / Means / Method: Pinpoints the physical tool, vehicle, language, or resource used to accomplish an action.
  • Total Constraints: Marks boundaries of cost, time tracking, or group composition.
 Uchi で tabemasu.
(I eat at home.)

Basu で ikimasu.
(I go by bus.)

Zenbu で sen-en desu.
(In total, it is 1,000 yen.)
へ (he)
  • Directional Path: Focuses purely on the directional trajectory or heading path toward a general area (pronounced 'e').
 Kyoto へ mukaimasu.
(I am heading toward Kyoto.)

Nihon へ no tabi.
(The journey toward Japan.)
と (to)
  • Exhaustive Linker: Connects nouns in a strict, fully completed list ("and").
  • Companion: Specifies the person or animal doing an action along with you ("with").
  • Quotation Marker: Encloses spoken thoughts, explicit quotes, or statements before verbs like iu (say) or omou (think).
 Pen と kami.
(A pen and paper. [And nothing else])

Tomodachi と aimashita.
(I met up with a friend.)

Ashita wa ame da と omoimasu.
(I think that it will rain tomorrow.)
の (no)
  • Possession / Relationship: Links nouns together to show ownership, descriptive attributes, or item placement.
  • Noun Modifier: Acts as a connector making the first noun modify or classify the second noun.
 Anata の kuruma.
(Your car.)

Nihongo の sensei.
(A teacher of Japanese / Japanese teacher.)
も (mo)
  • Inclusion Parallel: Adds parallel elements matching a previous context ("also", "too", "neither").
  • Emphasis Extent: Emphasizes an unexpectedly large quantity or extent ("as many as").
 Kare mo sensei desu.
(He is also a teacher.)

Neko ga jikki mo imasu.
(There are as many as 10 cats!)
から (kara)
  • Starting Point: Marks the physical origin point or starting time boundary ("from / since").
  • Reasoning: Placed cleanly at the end of a clause to state a logical cause ("because / so").
 Hachiji から benkyou shimasu.
(I study from 8:00.)

Atsui から mizu wo nomimasu.
(Because it is hot, I will drink water.)
まで (made)
  • Ending Limit: Marks the ultimate target destination, boundary, or time limit ("until / up to").
 Shibuya まで arukimasu.
(I will walk up to Shibuya.)

Kuji まで hatarakimasu.
(I work until 9:00.)
か (ka)
  • Question Flag: Placed at the very end of sentences to denote an explicit inquiry or doubt.
  • Alternative Selection: Placed between words to offer multiple choices ("or").
 Kore desu か？
(Is it this one?)

Inu か neko wo kaimasu.
(I will buy a dog or a cat.)
ね (ne)
  • Agreement Tag: Placed at sentence endings to seek validation, comfort, or shared consensus ("isn't it?", "right?").
 Ii tenki desu ね.
(Beautiful weather, right?)

Oishii desu ね.
(This tastes delicious, doesn't it?)
よ (yo)
  • Conviction Tag: Sentence-final emphasis marker used to safely deliver brand new info or advice with strong assertion ("I tell you!", "you know!").
 Kore wa oishii desu よ!
(This is delicious, I tell you!)

Jikan ga arimasen よ.
(You're out of time, you know.)
より (yori)
  • Comparison Anchor: Serves as the base point of comparison when measuring variables ("than").
 Kuruma より densha ga hayai desu.
(Trains are faster than cars.)

Kino より kyou wa atsui desu.
(Today is hotter than yesterday.)
ほど (hodo)
  • Negative Extent Limit: Used in negative comparison sentences to specify "not as much as."
  • Approximation: Describes an approximate measurement, duration, or degree ("about / roughly").
 Kesa wa kyou ほど samukunai desu.
(This morning wasn't as cold as today.)

Ichijikan ほど machimashita.
(I waited for about an hour.)
しか (shika)
  • Exclusive Restriction: Means "nothing else but" and **absolutely must** pair with negative verb endings to convey scarcity.
 Mizu しか arimasen.
(There is nothing but water.)

Sen-en しか mottimasen.
(I have no more than a thousand yen.)
だけ (dake)
  • Absolute Bound: Means "only / just" to establish an objective limit. Unlike *shika*, it normally pairs with affirmative, positive verb endings.
 Ringo だけ tabemashita.
(I only ate an apple.)

Sukoshi だけ wakarimasu.
(I understand just a little bit.)
など (nado)
  • Non-Exhaustive Listing: Implies that the items listed are mere examples and that other unmentioned things exist in the group ("and things like...", "etc.").
 Hon ya pen など wo kaimashita.
(I bought books, pens, and things like that.)

Yasai ya kudamono など ga suki desu.
(I like vegetables, fruits, etc.)
